# Job Automation

*This page is co-authored by the groups working on the Job Automation challenge: Abisko, Padjelanta, Skuleskogen, Ängsö.*

## Problem framing

Job automation refers to the use of digital systems — increasingly powered by artificial intelligence and machine learning — to perform tasks that were previously carried out by humans. Unlike earlier waves of automation that primarily displaced routine physical labour, today's AI-driven tools are encroaching on cognitive work: drafting documents, analysing data, supporting decisions, and even generating code or creative output. This challenge focuses on how these shifts are reshaping work across a wide range of roles, from knowledge workers and managers to manual workers and new labour market entrants.

The stakes are high and contested. Automation has the potential to raise productivity, reduce drudgery, and open up new kinds of work — but it can also accelerate inequality, erode job security, and leave large groups of workers without a clear path forward. Whether the dominant narrative is augmentation (AI as a tool that enhances human capacity) or replacement (AI substituting human labour) is not a neutral technical question; it reflects design choices, economic incentives, and political decisions about who bears the costs and who captures the gains.

Within this challenge, solutions can take many forms. A group might build a tool that helps workers adapt to automation, a training programme that supports skill transitions, a policy intervention that shapes how organisations deploy AI, or a data and measurement system that makes the effects of automation more legible. The common thread is that all approaches must grapple with a real tension: automation is already happening, and the question is not whether to engage with it, but how — and for whom.
